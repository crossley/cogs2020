Assumptions: single-sample t-test

  • Independence: The observations are independent of each other. This means that one participants score does not influence anothers.

  • Normality: The population from which the sample is drawn is normally distributed. This assumption becomes less critical with larger sample sizes due to the Central Limit Theorem.

  • Scale of measurement: The dependent variable is measured on a continuous (interval or ratio) scale.

Independence assumption

  • This assumption is mostly justified by study design.
  • Each observation should be unrelated to others.
  • Typical signs of potential dependence:
    • Repeated measures (unless accounted for)
    • Clustered data (e.g., students within a class)
    • Time-ordered data (e.g., learning trials)
  • Not directly testable from data in most simple designs.

Normality assumption

  • Can be inspected visually or tested statistically.
  • Visual checks:
    • Histogram: should look roughly bell-shaped
    • Q-Q plot: points fall on a straight diagonal line
  • Statistical tests:
    • Shapiro-Wilk test (preferred for small samples)
    • Anderson-Darling or Kolmogorov-Smirnov (alternatives)
  • Large samples (e.g., \(n \geq 30\)) reduce concern due to CLT.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

  • As \(n\) increases, the sampling distribution of the mean approaches normality, even if the population is non-normal.
  • This explains why t-tests can work well with moderate violations of normality.
  • But: CLT applies to means, not raw data!

When CLT is slow to help

  • If the population is skewed or has outliers, the CLT convergence to normality is slower.
  • This means larger samples are required for the t-test to remain valid.

Key takeaways

  • The t-test assumes independence, normality, and continuous measurement.
  • Independence is handled via good design.
  • Normality can be inspected visually and tested, but is less critical at large \(n\).
  • CLT justifies robustness of the t-test but with skew or outliers, you may need much larger samples.

Alternatives when assumptions are violated

  • Use nonparametric tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank test) when:
    • Sample size is small
    • Distribution is very skewed
    • There are extreme outliers
  • Use robust methods:
    • Trimmed means
    • Bootstrapping

Summary slide

  • The t-test is robust to many violations thanks to the CLT.
  • But robustness has limits:
    • Outliers and heavy skew can distort results unless \(n\) is large.
    • Always check assumptions before relying on a parametric test.
  • When in doubt: inspect, justify, or choose robust alternatives.
