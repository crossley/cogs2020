Introduction to Correlation

  • What is correlation?

  • It measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables.

  • It’s represented by the correlation coefficient, typically denoted as \(r\).

Visualizing Correlation

  • Scatter plots are a common tool to visualize the relationship between two variables.

Calculating Correlation Coefficient

  • Pearson’s correlation coefficient is calculated as:

\[ r = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2}} \]

  • Here’s how you compute it in R:
## [1] 0.7638609

Significance of the Correlation Coefficient

  • How do we interpret the value of \(r\)?

  • A rule of thumb:

    • \(|r| > 0.8\) : Very strong relationship
    • \(0.5 < |r| \leq 0.8\) : Strong relationship
    • \(0.3 < |r| \leq 0.5\) : Moderate relationship
    • \(0.1 < |r| \leq 0.3\) : Weak relationship
    • \(|r| \leq 0.1\) : Very weak or no relationship

Key Takeaway

  • Correlation is a fundamental statistical tool in data analysis.

  • Understanding its value and how to compute it can help identify trends and relationships in data.

  • Future lectures will delve deeper into how correlation affects model building and data interpretation.
